Why Did I Start Working on a Mineral Prospectivity Mapping Project?

My master's thesis made me venture on the world of programming. Well, I always liked knowing how to make computer programs and was always fascinated with tech. But I never had the chance to learn to do so. I studied mining engineering both for B.Sc and M.Sc. In B.Sc. we had a programming course which was pure non-sense. We were taught C++ and learned barely anything. But my master's thesis was a golden opportunity to learn Python.

Learning python was a great challenge. Not because I didn't know the language itself, but because I didn't understand the underlying concepts. And in those days, there was no AI-coding chatbot to give you help; it was all that bittersweet stackoverflow research and query. It took me a very long time to write the initial version of my thesis code. Now it lives as a python package called MinexPy. I had high hopes and big dreams for it. I wanted to turn it into a full-fledged python + MPM package that researchers all over the world could use. But I didn't publish it publicly until February 2026.

MinexPy could be a game changer for MPM researchers. However, I published it so late. I could've done that in 2021 and it would've been welcomed by the community. But I didn't do it. I don't know why. I think I didn't do it because it was too complex for me to understand what a package was and how it worked. Finally, in Februrary 2026 I spent a weekend turning that Jupyter Notebook into a package, and then, publishing it publically. I just wish I had done that in 2021 when it could have been actually useful to some people.

A Debt to Myself

I always thought that I hadn't done my degrees justice by using my programming skills and knowledge on subjects almost completely irrelevent to the topic of my studies. When I started publishing my thesis as MinexPy, I felt that I could do more. There were still topics in geosciences that I was keen on exploring, and problems I saw as oppotunities for doing research. One area, which was also related to my thesis, was mineral prospectivity mapping. What if I did MPM on some data and used machine learning for doing so? Bingo! That was a great challenge baby. Let's gooooooo. And on I went to learn machine learning, and also refresh my MPM knowledge.

But doing something you think you know, after almost 5 years of being out of touch, is much easier said than done. I started with the basics. First, I found a good online course to learn the basics of machine learning. Then, I tried formulating how ML could be used in geosciences. I chose a public dataset. I studied the dataset. I had to learn how to inspect it. So, I chose QGIS as the GIS software of choice, mainly because it was free and open-source. I had to learn how to navigate in QGIS, so I learned that too. Whoof, it has been hell of a challenge so far. I even hadn't started the ML pipeline up until then!

I finally managed to start writing the code for an end to end pipeline in May 2026 with the help of ChatGPT Codex 5.3. The first run was too good to be true. I chose the Northern Territory region of Australia to model MVT Zn-Pb deposits using random forest. The outcome was really good. I suspected overfitting. So, I started adding another layer of control over the model with asking questions about decisions I could have made, but I had passed. Right now, I'm there, doing that part.

The Finish Line

This project is giving me thesis vibes. My thesis was so interesting to me at first. But after a very short while the enthusiam was worn off and I could barely even type and document my research. I'm at that stage now. I love doing this project. But the enthusiam is wearing off. I think this happens to me because I'm overwhelmed with making too many decisions about things I do not know for sure. The finish line for this project is not in sight. But I need to preserve enough momentum that I can drag myself to the end. Whatever the reason for losing interest is, I need to finish it off very fast, before I become comepletely uninterested in this project.
